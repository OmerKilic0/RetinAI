Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy is commonly seen in individuals with long-term diabetes and often progresses without showing any symptoms. Our goal is to make an early diagnosis to prevent vision loss in individuals. Early intervention is crucial both for individual health and for reducing the burden on the healthcare system.

Frequently Asked Questions

Learn more about diabetic retinopathy through these common questions.

What is diabetic retinopathy?

Diabetic retinopathy is an eye disease caused by damage to the blood vessels in the retina due to high blood sugar levels in people with diabetes. It can lead to vision loss if not treated.

What are the symptoms of diabetic retinopathy?

In the early stages, it may not show any symptoms. As it progresses, symptoms may include blurred vision, dark or empty areas in the visual field, faded colors, and sudden vision loss.

Who is at risk for diabetic retinopathy?

People with Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes, those who have had diabetes for a long time, individuals with poor blood sugar control, and those with high blood pressure or high cholesterol are at higher risk.

How is diabetic retinopathy diagnosed?

It is diagnosed by an eye doctor through a detailed eye examination (fundus examination), optical coherence tomography (OCT), or fluorescein angiography.

How is diabetic retinopathy treated?

Treatment options include laser therapy, intraocular injections (anti-VEGF), and vitrectomy surgery. These treatments can slow the progression and prevent vision loss.

How can diabetic retinopathy be prevented?

Controlling blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol levels, eating a healthy diet, regular exercise, avoiding smoking, and having annual eye exams can help prevent diabetic retinopathy.
